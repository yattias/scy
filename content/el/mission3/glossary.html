Glossary

Absorption. The uptake of nutrients by the wall of the small and large intestine.

Anus. Opening of the rear end of the digestive canal, used to expel faeces (shit). The anus is controlled by a circular muscle.

Arteriosclerosis: is a general term describing the hardening (and loss of elasticity) of medium or large arteries.

Basal metabolic rate (BMR). The amount of energy you spend when your body is at rest. This is the energy required for the functioning of your vital organs (e.g. your heart, lungs, kidneys, brain, etc.).

Blood pressure: is the pressure exerted by circulating blood on the walls of blood vessels.

Body mass index (BMI). A measure that is often used to estimate a (healthy) body weight given the height of a person.

Calorie. The energy in food is measured in calories or joules. A calorie is defined as the amount of energy that is needed to increase the temperature of 1 ml of water by 1 ˚C. One calorie equals 4.2 joule. One kilocalorie (kcal) is one thousand calories.

Calorimeter: is a device used for calorimetry, the science of measuring the heat of chemical reactions or physical changes as well as heat capacity.

Cardiovascular diseases. Diseases that involve the heart and/or the blood vessels. Atherosclerosis (thickening of the artery wall) is a very common cardiovascular disease, which may result in a heart attack or stroke.

Cholesterol: It is an essential structural component of mammalian cell membranes, where it is required to establish proper membrane permeability and fluidity. Although cholesterol is an important and necessary molecule for animals, a high level of serum cholesterol is an indicator for diseases such as heart disease.

Colon: is the last part of the digestive system in most vertebrates. It extracts water, salt, potassium and some fat soluble vitamins from solid wastes before they are eliminated from the body.

Deep pan pizza: This pizza is unique because it is far from the roots of an original Italian pizza. It does not include thin crusts or delicate toppings, but rather it is made with a heavy, thick crust and large amounts of cheese, sauce and ingredients.

Diabetes. Disease in which the regulation of blood sugar concentration is disturbed. An unhealthy lifestyle (eating too much fat and sugar, in combination with a lack of regular exercise) may cause diabetes type 2 (a specific type of diabetes).

Digestive juices. Secretions of the organs of the digestive system that break down your food into nutrients that are small enough to be absorbed by the intestine. Examples of digestive juices are saliva (produced in the mouth), gastric juice (produced in the stomach) and pancreatic juice (produced in the pancreas).

Digestive system. The digestive system consists of all the organs that are involved in digestion (the breakdown of food and the uptake of nutrients). In humans, the digestive system consists of the mouth, the oesophagus, the stomach, the duodenum, the liver, the gall bladder, the small intestine, the large intestine, the rectum and the anus.

Domestic animals: to be considered domesticated, a population of animals must have their behavior, life cycle, or physiology systemically altered as a result of being under human control for many generations.

Dough: is a paste made out of any cereals (grains) or leguminous crops by mixing flour with a small amount of water and/or other liquid.

Duodenum. The first section of the small intestine, directly connected to the stomach. The pancreas and the gall bladder release their digestive juices in the digestive tract in the duodenum.

Enzymes. Special substances in digestive juices that help to break down large nutrients into smaller components. Each nutrient is broken down by a another, specific enzyme.

Estimated energy requirements (EER). The total amount of energy that your body needs. The main components of the EER are the basal metabolic rate (BMR) and the energy that is used for the activities your body undertakes.

Food pyramid: provides optimal nutrition guidelines for each food category, per day, using a mnemonic graphic of a pyramid with vertical dividing lines to represent suggested percentages of the daily diet for each food group.

Fowl: is a word for birds in general but usually refers to birds belonging to one of two biological orders, namely the game fowl or land fowl and the waterfowl.

Fuel consumption: a process that converts chemical potential energy contained in a carrier fuel into kinetic energy or work. Overall fuel efficiency may vary per device.

Gall bladder. The gall bladder stores bile, which is produced by the liver. Bile helps the breakdown of fats in your food.

Grains: the small, hard, fruits or seeds of arable crops or the crops bearing these fruits or seeds.

Heart rate. The number of heart beats per minute.

Joule. The energy in food is measured in joules or calories. One joule equals 0.24 calorie. One kilojoule (kJ) is one thousand joules.

Large intestine. The last section of the digestive canal before the rectum and the anus. In the large intestine, water is absorbed from the remaining matter in the digestive tract.

Larynx: commonly called the voice box, is an organ in the neck of mammals involved in protecting the trachea and sound production. The larynx houses the vocal folds, which are an essential component of phonation.

Lipids: are a broad group of naturally occurring molecules which includes fats, waxes, fat-soluble vitamins (such as vitamins A, D, E and K), and others. The main biological functions of lipids include energy storage, as structural components of cell membranes, and as important signaling molecules.

Liver. The liver is a large, brown organ and has many functions. For instance, the liver produces bile, and detoxifies the blood with nutrients coming from the intestines.

Mouth. The beginning of the digestive canal. In the mouth the teeth are used to chew your food. In addition, saliva is added.

Obesity. A condition in which people are overweight to an extent that their health is reduced. People with a body mass index over 30 kg/m2 are considered obese.

Esophagus. The first section of the digestive canal after the mouth. The oesophagus transports everything you swallow to your stomach.

Pancreas. The pancreas is a gland that produces pancreatic juice, which is released in the duodenum.

Peristalsis. The rhythmic contraction of muscles of the wall of the digestive tract. Food is pushed forward through the digestive canal by peristalsis.

Pharynx: is the part of the throat situated behind the mouth and nasal cavity. The pharynx is part of the digestive system and also the respiratory system; it is also important in vocalization.

Prostate: a compound gland of the male reproductive system in most mammals.

Rectum. The last section of the digestive canal, in which faeces (shit) is temporarily stored.

Small intestine. The longest section of the digestive canal, in between the stomach and the large intestine. In human adults, the small intestine has a length of about five metres. In the small intestine, nutrients are absorbed and enter the bloodstream.

Stomach. A large, muscular section of the digestive canal. In the stomach, food is churned and mixed with gastric juice. Gastric juice contains enzymes (to break down proteins) and acids (to kill bacteria and other micro organisms).

Stuffed crust pizza: a style of pizza with a crust usually stuffed with cheese.

Thin crust pizza: a style of pizza with a thin and firm crust, enough to have a noticeable crunch.

Unhealthy food: foods which are perceived to have little or no nutritional value, or products with nutritional value but which also have ingredients considered unhealthy when regularly eaten or unhealthy to consume at all.

World Health Organization: is a specialized agency of the United Nations (UN) that acts as a coordinating authority on international public health.
