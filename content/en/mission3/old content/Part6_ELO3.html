6.3 Health passport

You are now going to finish the health passport for the character you have chosen. This health passport brings together the most important facts regarding his/her diet and health. During previous activities you have already calculated the daily calorie intake, BMR and EER of your character. You have also evaluated his/her diet. To complete the health passport, you will calculate the body mass index (BMI), enter a realistic value for the heart rate and write some conclusions and recommendations.

Body Mass Index
The Body Mass Index (BMI) is used to indicate whether someone has a healthy body weight. You probably remember the dietician measuring Morgan’s bodyweight in ‘Super Size Me’. To calculate the BMI, you need to know the weight (in kilograms) and the height (in metres) of a person.

If you do not know how to use this formula, follow these steps:

  • Take the height of your character in meters.
  • Multiply this value by itself.
  • Divide the weight of your character (in kg) by the outcome of the previous step.
  • The outcome is the BMI.

2. Enter the BMI of your character in the health passport [link to SCYLab tool, see appendix A].

Now that you have calculated the BMI, you probably want to know what this value says about physical health. There are roughly four different categories:

Underweight                 your BMI is under 18.5
Normal weight              your BMI is in between 18.5 and 25
Overweight                   your BMI is in between 25 and 30
Obese                           your BMI is over 30

If you would like to know your own BMI, you can use the same formula of course. But before jumping to conclusions, you should be aware that these categories normally apply to adults. Your body is still developing and growing, and your BMI might change considerably over the years.

Heart rate
You have seen the physicians that were monitoring Morgan’s health in ‘Super Size Me’ measuring his heart rate and blood pressure. These values might provide information about a person’s health. Even though we are not physicians, and therefore cannot make a reliable medical interpretation, it might be interesting to measure our heart rate as well.

Heart rate is measured in beats per minute (bpm). You probably know that your heart beats a lot faster when you are exercising. For the health passport we need the resting heart rate. This is the heart rate when a person is awake, but not doing any physical work. A healthy resting heart rate will be somewhere in between 60 and 80 bpm for adults. For teenagers, the outer limits of this healthy range are set even further apart. Resting heart rates of 90 bpm are no exception. But heart rates below 60 bpm are also common, especially if you exercise regularly.

1. Enter a realistic value for the heart rate of your character in the health passport [link to SCYLab tool, see appendix A]. As a (very) general rule you might say that the resting heart rate becomes lower if a person exercises more regularly.

Measuring your own heart rate
There are a number of places on your body where you can feel your own pulse. A good place to start looking for it would be your neck, where you probably will be able to find the pulse of the so called carotid artery just below your chin (see the drawing below).

FIGURE 3

  • Sit down and relax your muscles. Place two fingers of one hand just below your chin and try to find your pulse. Hold a stopwatch in your other hand. Once you have found your pulse, start the stopwatch and count the number of heartbeats for one minute.
  • Ask two of your fellow students to measure your heart rate as well.
  • Calculate the average of these three measurements. This value will be a reasonably good estimate of your heart rate.
